Palermo doesn't ease you in. Markets roar, scooters weave, and the smell of fried panelle fills every alley. It's Sicily's capital in every sense — loud, generous, ancient, and utterly itself. Arab-Norman palaces sit beside Baroque churches; lunch costs five euros at a market stall and fifty at a courtyard osteria.

This is the Mediterranean at its most visceral. No polish, no performance — just extraordinary food, layered history, and a city that reminds you life is meant to be tasted, not scheduled. Tutto passa, but Palermo stays with you.
