The Mediterranean isn't the Pacific. The waves are smaller, the swells less predictable, the culture less commercial. And for thousands of surfers across Southern Europe, that's exactly the point.

## A different kind of surf town

Med surf towns don't look like Bali or Byron Bay. There's no surf shop on every corner. The breaks are often rocky, the water warm, the sessions shared with fishermen and swimmers. In Ericeira, Portugal — Europe's only World Surfing Reserve — you surf in the morning and eat grilled sardines at lunch. In Hossegor, France, Atlantic swells meet Landes pine forests. In Crete, you might be the only board in the water.

The culture is intimate. Locals know their spots, their seasons, their wind patterns. There's less performance, more pleasure. Less "shredding," more riding.

## Where to start

**Beginners:** Costa da Caparica (Lisbon), Matosinhos (Porto), Barceloneta (Barcelona on small days)

**Intermediate:** Ericeira, Taghazout (Morocco — technically Atlantic but Med-adjacent culture), Fuerteventura

**Adventure:** Hidden coves in Sardinia, Albania's unspoiled coast, western Crete

## The lifestyle connection

Surfing in the Mediterranean isn't about chasing the biggest wave. It's about starting the day in the water, salt on your skin, coffee tasting better afterward. It's a reason to wake early, to read the wind, to know a coastline intimately.

The best surf towns share something with the best coastal towns: community, simplicity, and the understanding that some mornings are meant for the sea.

*Everything passes* — including the set. Paddle back out.
