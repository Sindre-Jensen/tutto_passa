At 1:30pm on a Wednesday in Palermo, a restaurant fills with locals. Not tourists. Not business lunches with laptops. Families, colleagues, friends — settling in for two, sometimes three hours of eating, talking, and sitting in the shade.

This is lunch as the Mediterranean understands it: the main event of the day.

## Structure without rigidity

A long lunch has a rhythm, not a script:

1. **Antipasti** — olives, bread, something fried, something cured. Wine opens.
2. **Primo** — pasta, risotto, soup. The conversation deepens.
3. **Secondo** — fish or meat, if appetite allows. Often skipped.
4. **Dolce** — coffee, perhaps dessert. The table negotiates the end.

Nobody rushes between courses. The waiter doesn't hover with the bill. Time expands.

## Why it survives

Globalisation was supposed to kill the long lunch. Deadlines, Zoom calls, fast casual. And yet in Spain, Italy, Greece, Portugal — it persists. Because it works.

Long lunches are where deals are made, families stay connected, and the social fabric holds. They're networking without LinkedIn. Therapy without a therapist. Rest without guilt.

## A lunch worth having

You don't need three hours every day. You need one lunch a week where:

- The phone stays in your bag
- The table is shared with someone you like
- The food is local and unhurried
- Nobody checks the time

Find a restaurant with shaded terrace, order too much, and let the afternoon dissolve. The Mediterranean figured this out centuries ago. We're just catching up.
