UNESCO lists the Mediterranean diet as intangible cultural heritage. Nutritionists praise its heart-health benefits. Cookbook publishers sell millions of olive oil-forward recipes.

But the diet was never really about food.

## Eat together, eat slowly

The research is consistent: Mediterraneans who live longest don't eat alone in front of screens. They eat at tables, with others, over conversation. The social component isn't a footnote — it may be the mechanism.

Meals are events, not refuelling stops. Even a simple lunch of bread, tomatoes, and cheese becomes something more when shared on a terrace overlooking the sea.

## Move without "exercise"

Mediterranean populations walk constantly — to market, to work, to the evening passeggiata. They garden. They swim. They climb hills to villages without thinking of it as a workout.

The movement is embedded in geography and habit, not gym memberships. Stairs instead of elevators. Walking to the bakery. Swimming because it's hot and the sea is there.

## Seasonal, local, enough

The diet's ingredients are famous: olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, moderate wine. Less famous is the principle behind them: eat what's growing now, from nearby, until you're satisfied — not stuffed.

Waste is minimal. Preservation (curing, drying, fermenting) extends seasons. Every part of the animal or vegetable finds a use. This isn't austerity — it's respect.

## The lesson

You can eat olive oil in London and live longer. But the full Mediterranean diet requires Mediterranean life: sun, community, slowness, pleasure. The food is the entry point. The lifestyle is the destination.

*Tutto passa* — but a good meal with people you love? That stays.
