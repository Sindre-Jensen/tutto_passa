In Italy, espresso isn't a drink. It's a ritual — brief, precise, and performed the same way millions of times each morning from Palermo to Trieste.

You enter the bar. You greet the bartender. You order "un caffè" — never an espresso, which would mark you as foreign. You stand at the counter. You drink it in three sips, perhaps four. You pay (or you've already paid at the register). You leave. Total time: ninety seconds. Total satisfaction: disproportionate.

## The bar as social infrastructure

Italian bars are democratic spaces. The lawyer in a suit stands beside the construction worker. Everyone drinks the same coffee from the same machine. There's no table service for espresso — standing keeps it fast, cheap, and equal.

This isn't rudeness. It's respect for the ritual. Sitting down changes the economics and the energy. At the bar, coffee is fuel and greeting combined. At the table, it becomes something else — leisure, conversation, time stretching out.

## The craft behind the speed

Don't mistake speed for carelessness. A proper Italian espresso requires:

- Fresh beans, ground moments before extraction
- The right pressure, temperature, and timing
- A warm cup, a golden crema, a volume of roughly 25ml

The best bars pull hundreds of espressos daily, each one consistent. The skill is invisible because it has to be — the ritual demands reliability, not performance.

## How to drink like a local

1. **Stand at the bar** — it's cheaper and more authentic
2. **Skip the afternoon** — after 11am, many Italians switch to macchiato or stop entirely
3. **Never order a cappuccino after 11am** — the unwritten rule tourists learn the hard way
4. **Don't linger** — the bar is for flow, not lounging

The espresso is Italy in miniature: intense, brief, perfect, and gone before you can overthink it. *Tutto passa* — even the best coffee lasts only a moment. That's what makes it worth savouring.
